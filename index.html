Crafting a resume
  • A properly written resume takes A LOT of time to write
    • Not an afternoon project
    • There is always room to improve
  • You are your resume
    • Employers do not know anything about you but what you have written
    • Ask yourself: "Would I want to know more about me? Is this interesting?"
  • Preparing your resume means preparing yourself
    • Get technical exposure outside of classwork
    • Explore personal projects, contribute to open source
Takes a lot of time: Your resume is extremely important. It is the only thing that can get you in the door to a completely unknown environment. It is the single most important artifact to you during your job search. I cannot stress enough just how important it is. The content, the format, the words you use, the way you describe things, the way you prioritize things, the inflexions, the grammar, everything. Everything you have in your resume matters. Everything NEEDS to be carefully considered and calculated down the smallest detail. Your resume is NOT an afternoon, or even a weekend project. Your resume is, in a way, a lifetime project, because your resume is you. It is a compilation of everything you are, and because of that, you cannot compromise any part of it. Which is why you cannot spend just an afternoon on it. It needs to be done very, very deliberately. It needs to be read, reread, proof read, formatted, reformatted, spacing needs to be well done, it needs to be attractive. I should be able to look at your resume and tell that you have poured time into it. Because if I am looking at your resume, and it is not put together well, what confidence have you given me that you are any better? You are your resume: Your resume is you. I, as your potential employer, know absolutely nothing about you besides what is written on your resume. Your resume is an ad and you are the product. You need to make me want you. You need to show me that you are valuable, you need to make me want more. It is perfectly to be terse. The idea is to start a story, and let them want to know more. I have seen it, I have read resume’s where they will describe a project and I will think it’s cool, and will interview based purely on that. I am not saying to artificially fabricate a fairy tale, no, make what you have done interesting, and most of all valuable. Prepare yourself: Because your resume is you, it can only ever be as good as you are. But this is a limitation on the resume, not you. Make yourself better, and update your resume to reflect that. You guys are all U of M student, with the courses you have taken, you all virtually the exact same expertise. What differentiates you from everyone else is what you have beyond your normal schoolwork. It is not hard to learn a new technology. You need to expose yourself to technologies outside your normal classwork. You need to have personal projects, and even contribute to open source if you can. You are only as strong as you make yourself. I challenge everyone here to take one weekend a month to learn something new. I understand that everyone is busy, but take the time. Find something you want to learn about, find an interesting technology, and spend a day or two learning it. You do not have to be an expert in it, just working knowledge is good. .NET and GIT are widely used technologies, and guess what? They are free, go learn them. Like I said, your resume is what gets you in the door, it is important, you need to put in the time and effort to make it achieve its goal.

Must have passion

  • Strive for excellence
  • Be passionate
    • Continuing education
    • Conferences, local meet-up groups
    • Learn and understand your industry
  • Am I adding value?
Mohammed --Passion-- One of the points we want to make here is that you must be passionate about what you do. Strive for excellence, and you will be successful. Continue your education. Do not stop learning just because you are out of school. The tech industry is massive, and I argue that schooling does not even scratch the surface of what technology really is. You need to take a shovel and start digging, start learning more and more. Show passion for what you do, and learn any and everything. You can do this by going to conferences and meeting up with local dev groups. Ann Arbor has one, as do most communities. Coffee house coders anyone? Conferences, anyone go to 1DevDay in Detroit? Excellent conference with a lot of great new material, and I learned a lot. Great way to source as well. Learn your industry. This is will only benefit you. The better you understand the industry you work in, the more valuable you are, and with that always ask yourself, “Am I adding value”. I ask myself this all the time. Am I adding value? And what can I do to add more. You are more than disposable labor, you are an asset, and you should continue to be so.
Develop soft skills

  • Get to know your coworkers
  • Interpersonal skills
  • Conflict resolution
Mohammed --Soft Skills-- And on a final note, in the working environment, soft skills are a must. Do not just show up, do your forty hours, and leave. Your career will be the single most time consuming activity in your life. Even more so than sleep. Forty hours minimum. There are one hundred sixty eight hours in a week and more than a quarter of your time will go into spending it working. I do not want that to sound somber, but it is true. You should take every effort to make it as enjoyable as possible. Get to know your coworkers. I have a personal to semi-personal relationship with virtually every person at my company. Your interpersonal skills are extremely important, despite hating group projects in school, the reality is, there is a reason they make you do them. In life, seldom will you ever be the only person working on a project. Large projects will create teams of tens of hundreds of people for a single effort. You need to be able to get along and work with other people. Conflicts will happen, and it is extremely important that you address them in a professional manner. These skills will all help you.
